There's infatuation, there's attraction, there's love...

...and then, there's limerence.

↓ ↓ ↓

What it is

Limerence is an intense, involuntary state of romantic infatuation marked by obsessive longing for another person and a powerful need for reciprocity. This desire appears uninvited; it feels unintentional, as if someone bewitched you. Think of puppy love that lingers and gets out of control. Billie Holiday's song You Go To My Head is that feeling in pure form; her description is textbook limerence:

↓ ↓ ↓

You go to my head

And you linger like a haunting refrain

And I find you spinnin' 'round in my brain

Like the bubbles in a glass of champagne

↓ ↓ ↓

What it feels like

If you recognize this feeling, know that those deep and intense desires are fundamental to our nature. This phenomenon happens in three phases: Infatuation, Crystallisation, and Deterioration.

In the 1930's Billie Holiday was probably going through the Infatuation and Crystallisation stages, while in 2022 Bad Bunny might have been transitioning from Crystallisation to Deterioration when he wrote Un Coco:

↓ ↓ ↓

Me paso fumando bajo las palmera'

(I spend my time smoking under the palm trees)

A ver si me cae un coco en la cabeza y me borra mi vida entera

(To see if a coconut falls on my head and erases my whole life)

Veo parejitas dándose beso' y me da dentera

(I see couples kissing and it gives me the creeps)

Voy a tirarle el radio por mar si sale Sin Bandera porque

(I'm going to throw the radio into the sea if Sin Bandera comes on because)

No puedo escuchar cancione' de romance

(I can't listen to romantic songs)

Me cansé que de pensar en ti no me canse

(I'm tired of not getting tired of thinking about you)

El último pétalo me dijo que aún hay chance

(The last petal told me there's still a chance)

No sé si es verdad, pero si va' a volver, avanza

(I don't know if it's true, but if you're going to come back, go ahead)

Por favor

(Please)

↓ ↓ ↓

The Spell: Infatuation

As you can see, sometimes attraction feels like Cupid's arrow or a love spell: sudden, unintended, and impossible to reason with.

Limerence, as the intense longing for someone whose feelings we cannot fully know, takes over our thoughts as if they hold a secret we urgently need.

↓ ↓ ↓

The Movie inside your Head: Crystallisation

But look closely. That turmoil is happening inside Billie's mind, Bad Bunny's mind, and yours. In reality, it is your thoughts and energy creating the emotional momentum, rarely the person themselves.

As Dorothy Tennov, the psychologist who coined the term, explains, this experience is a production staged entirely in your head:

↓ ↓ ↓

Limerence is, above all else, mental activity. It is an interpretation of events, rather than the events themselves. You admire, you are physically attracted, you see, or think you see (or deem it possible to see under “suitable” conditions), the hint of possible reciprocity, and the process is set in motion.

↓ ↓ ↓

The Realization: Deterioration

But what if the “beloved” is not the main character, but a supporting role we created to look back at ourselves?

Some psychologists describe limerence as a projection of our unacknowledged qualities, unmet needs, and internal stories. We don't really see them, but a symbol: an image shaped by unconscious desires, attachment patterns, and the idealized narratives we carry. We fall in love with the “missing half” of our own psyche.

In limerence, we may actually be in love with our own unrealized potential, reflected through another.

↓ ↓ ↓

An Invitation

Instead of trying to possess the person who awakens this feeling, we can turn the attraction inward and treat it as a message from the parts of us that seek expression.

Acknowledging that the hidden aspects of yourself are projecting themselves onto others can serve as a form of self-discovery, creating a space where desire can return to its source: yourself
