Sometimes attraction feels like a love spell or a Cupid's arrow that hit the target: sudden, unintended, and impossible to reason with. Limerence, as the intense longing for someone whose feelings we cannot fully know or whose reciprocation we desperately want, can take over our thoughts as if they hold a secret we urgently need. But when you look closely, all of that is happening inside Bad Bunny's (and your) mind. In reality, it is your thoughts and energy that create that emotional momentum, rarely something external, including the object of your longing. As Dorothy Tennov, the psychologist who coined the term limerence, explains, this experience is happening mostly in your head:

Limerence is, above all else, mental activity. It is an interpretation of events, rather than the events themselves. You admire, you are physically attracted, you see, or think you see (or deem it possible to see under “suitable” conditions), the hint of possible reciprocity, and the process is set in motion.

But what if the “beloved” is not a main character, but a supporting role we created to look back at ourselves?

Some psychologists describe limerence as a projection of our unacknowledged qualities, unmet needs, and internal stories. We don't really see them, but a symbol: an image shaped by unconscious desires, attachment patterns, and the idealized narratives we carry. We fall in love with a “missing half” of our own psyche. In limerence, we may actually be in love with our own unrealized potential, reflected through another.

An Invitation

Instead of trying to possess the person who awakens this feeling, we can turn the attraction inward and treat it as a message from the parts of us that seek expression.
Acknowledging that the hidden aspects of yourself are projecting themselves onto others can serve as a form of self-discovery, creating a space where desire can return to its source: yourself

This experience invites you to do that. Think of someone who has stirred longing, admiration, confusion, gratitude, or limerent desire. Not necessarily a lover (it could be a friend, a fleeting crush, a situationship, a mentor, or a stranger who shifted something in you). You'll credit them as if they were part of the film of that chapter of your life.
